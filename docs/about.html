Key References

Foundational and methodological references that form the theoretical backbone of the research.

Reference List
[1]
Jones, S.R. (2016). When brain rhythms aren't 'rhythmic': implication for their mechanisms and meaning. Curr Opin Neurobiol.
Foundational for the study's core premise. It argues for analyzing brain oscillations as transient "bursts" or "events" rather than sustained rhythms, justifying the methodological shift from averaged power spectral density to event-based analysis.
[2]
Shin, H., et al. (2017). The rate of transient beta frequency events predicts behavior across tasks and species. eLife.
Provides the primary methodological framework. The study's spectral event detection algorithm and feature extraction (rate, duration, power, frequency span) are directly based on this work.
[3]
Pusil, S., López, M.E., et al. (2019). Hypersynchronization in mild cognitive impairment: The 'X' model. Brain.
Presents the key neuroanatomical model guiding the study. The "X" model proposes disrupted connectivity between the Anterior Cingulate Cortex (ACC) and Precuneus (PC) as a hallmark of MCI-to-AD conversion, which is why these two regions were selected as ROIs.
[4]
Lundqvist, M., et al. (2016, 2018, 2024). Various papers on beta/gamma bursts and working memory/cognitive control.
Critical for interpreting the results in the Discussion. These studies establish the link between beta events and inhibitory control, volitional command, and working memory, allowing the authors to frame their findings (reduced beta events) as a potential deficit in inhibitory cognitive control in AD.
[5]
Dauwels, J., et al. (2011). Slowing and loss of complexity in Alzheimer's EEG... Int J Alzheimers Dis.
These are classic, frequently cited papers that document the phenomenon of "oscillatory slowing" (increased low-frequency, decreased high-frequency power) in Alzheimer's disease and its prodromal stages, forming the background problem the study seeks to refine.
[6]
Babiloni, C., et al. (2004, 2009, 2010). Series on EEG rhythms in MCI/AD.
Classic references documenting oscillatory changes in Alzheimer's disease.
[7]
Jeong, J. (2004). EEG dynamics in patients with Alzheimer's disease. Clin Neurophysiol.
Classic reference on EEG dynamics in Alzheimer's disease.
[8]
Sherman, M.A., et al. (2016). Neural mechanisms of transient neocortical beta rhythms... Proc Natl Acad Sci USA.
Provides a biophysically principled, cross-species model for the generation of neocortical beta events via thalamocortical drive. This model is central to the mechanistic hypothesis proposed in the Discussion (Section 4.4) linking reduced beta events to thalamocortical drive deficit and hyperexcitability.
[9]
Maestú, F., et al. (2021). Neuronal excitation/inhibition imbalance: Core element of a translational perspective on Alzheimer pathophysiology. Ageing Res Rev.
Connects the study's findings to a major contemporary pathophysiological theory of AD—E/I imbalance and hyperexcitability—providing a broader disease context.

Brain Regions of Interest

Anatomical locations of the Anterior Cingulate Cortex (ACC) and Precuneus (PC) – the two key regions analyzed in the study. These hubs of the default mode network show early functional changes in Alzheimer's disease.

Power Spectral Density Comparison

Comparison of average power spectral density between CONV (converters) and NOCONV (non‑converters) groups, showing the characteristic "oscillatory slowing" – reduced beta power and increased low‑frequency power in converters.
