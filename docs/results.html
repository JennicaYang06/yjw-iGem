Key Statistical Results

Summary of the most significant findings from the MEG study on beta events as early biomarkers of Alzheimer's disease conversion.

  • Oscillatory Slowing Confirmed: CONV group showed decreased beta power (17-30 Hz in ACC, 11-27 Hz in PC) and increased low-frequency power (theta/low-alpha) compared to NOCONV.
  • Beta Events Underlie Averaged Power: Transient high-power beta events strongly correlated with averaged beta power (rate, duration, peak power).
  • Reduced Beta Event Features: CONV patients had significantly lower beta event rate and power in precuneus, shorter duration in ACC.
  • Predictive Biomarkers: Diminished beta event features predicted higher odds of AD conversion within 2.5 years (odds ratios up to 2.01).
  • Cognitive Correlation: Within CONV group, lower beta event rate and power correlated with poorer cognitive performance.
  • Alpha Events Not Discriminative: Despite group differences in averaged alpha power, alpha event features did not predict conversion.

Detailed Results

Comprehensive analysis of beta event features and their predictive value for Alzheimer's disease conversion.

3.1 AD converters exhibit oscillatory slowing in average PSD

The analysis of average power spectral density (PSD) confirmed the established phenomenon of oscillatory slowing in patients who later converted to Alzheimer's disease (AD). Compared to the non-converter (NOCONV) group, the CONV group demonstrated a statistically significant reduction in relative power within the beta frequency band. This decrease was observed in two key regions of interest (ROIs): the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC; 17-30 Hz) and the precuneus (PC; 11-27 Hz). Conversely, the CONV group exhibited a significant increase in relative power in lower frequency bands (theta/low-alpha) in both the ACC (5-7 Hz) and PC (4-8 Hz). These spectral differences are visually represented in Figure 2 Figure 2 , which plots normalized PSD for both groups with shaded areas indicating statistically significant clusters. Based on these spectral profiles, the subsequent event-based analysis focused on the [5-10] Hz band for alpha and the [12-30] Hz band for beta activity.

3.2 Resting-state alpha and beta emerge as transient high-power events

The study established that the seemingly continuous average power in the alpha and beta bands, as seen in standard PSD plots, originates from the temporal summation of transient, high-power oscillatory "events" in the unaveraged neural signal. Figure 3 Figure 3 provides time-frequency representations (TFRs) illustrating this: while the averaged TFR (top panels) shows sustained power, examination of individual, non-averaged trials (middle and bottom panels) reveals that this activity is composed of discrete, high-power bursts.

To quantify how these transient events contribute to the average power, regression analyses were performed. For the beta band ([12-30] Hz), there was a strong, significant positive correlation between averaged beta power and several event features: event number (rate; β = .28, p = .0088), event duration (β = .45, p < .0001), and event peak power (β = .41, p = .0001). A significant negative correlation was found with event frequency span (β = -.29, p = .0075). In contrast, for the alpha band ([5-10] Hz), only event frequency span showed a significant (but weaker) correlation with averaged alpha power after multiple comparison correction (β = -.25, p = .025). These relationships are detailed in Figure 4 Figure 4 .

3.3 Reduced beta event features as predictive biomarkers of AD conversion and cognitive decline

Crucially, the study identified specific deficits in transient beta event features that distinguish future AD converters (CONV) from non-converters (NOCONV). Figure 5 Figure 5 and the associated Table 2 present the key comparative statistics.

  • Group Differences in Beta Events: In the precuneus (PC), the CONV group had a significantly lower rate (number) of beta events (p = .001) and lower beta event power (p = .002) compared to the NOCONV group. A strong trend toward shorter beta event duration in the PC was also observed (p = .051). In the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC), beta event duration was significantly shorter in the CONV group (p = .002). The effect sizes (Cohen's d) for these differences were in the medium range (d > 0.4). No significant group differences were found for any alpha event features.
  • Prediction of AD Conversion: Logistic regression models revealed that diminished beta event features were associated with a higher probability of converting from MCI to AD within 2.5 years. Specifically:
    • MCI subjects with fewer than 4.92 beta events per 4-second epoch had 1.79 times greater odds of AD conversion.
    • Subjects with beta event durations shorter than 159 ms had 1.06 times greater odds.
    • Subjects with beta event peak power below 8.41 (in normalized FOM units) had 2.01 times greater odds.
    These predictive relationships are illustrated in Figure 6A Figure 6A .
  • Association with Cognitive Decline: Within the CONV group only, a lower rate of beta events and lower beta event power were linearly associated with a poorer Cognitive Performance Index (a composite score derived from neuropsychological tests). Specifically, for event number: β = .26, p = .016; for event power: β = .31, p = .0041. This relationship was not observed for event duration, nor was it present in the NOCONV group. The association between beta event features and cognitive performance is shown in Figure 6B Figure 6B .

In summary, the results demonstrate that the well-documented "slowing" of the neural oscillatory profile in prodromal AD is mechanistically linked to a reduction in the expression of transient, high-power beta band events (specifically, their rate, power, and duration) within hub regions of the default mode network. These specific beta event deficits not only differentiate future AD converters but also predict both a higher likelihood of clinical conversion and the degree of concurrent cognitive decline.
