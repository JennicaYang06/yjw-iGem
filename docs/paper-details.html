High-power transient beta events as early biomarkers of Alzheimer's disease conversion: An MEG study

A magnetoencephalography investigation of oscillatory events predicting progression from mild cognitive impairment to Alzheimer's disease

1. Abstract

This MEG study investigated transient high-power oscillatory "events" or "bursts" as potential biomarkers for predicting conversion from Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) to Alzheimer's Disease (AD). Analyzing resting-state data from MCI patients over 2.5 years, the researchers found that reduced expression of beta-band (12-30 Hz) events—specifically lower rate, power, and duration in the precuneus—was predictive of future AD conversion and cognitive decline. These event-based features offer a more precise interpretation of neural "slowing" than traditional averaged power metrics.

2. Introduction

Traditional analysis of MEG/EEG in AD focuses on oscillatory slowing, characterized by increased low-frequency and decreased high-frequency power, averaged over time. This averaging may mask important transient dynamics. Recent studies show neural oscillations often occur as transient high-power bursts. This study tested the hypothesis that features of these transient events, particularly in brain hubs of the AD-related "X" model (Anterior Cingulate Cortex - ACC, and Precuneus - PC), would differ between MCI patients who later convert to AD (CONV) and those who do not (NOCONV), and relate to cognitive decline.

3. Methods

Subjects: 85 MCI patients, later classified into CONV (N=41) and NOCONV (N=44) groups after 2.5 years. Groups were matched for age, sex, and education.

Data Acquisition: Resting-state MEG recordings were obtained.

Analysis:

  • Select regions of interest: Anterior Cingulate Cortex (ACC) and Precuneus (PC)
  • Compute Power Spectral Density (PSD) and Time-Frequency Representations
  • Detect high-power transient events (defined as local power maxima exceeding 6× median)
  • Extract event features: rate, duration, frequency span, and power

Statistics: Compared groups (CONV vs. NOCONV) on PSD and event features. Used regression models to link event features to AD conversion probability and a composite Cognitive Performance Index.

4. Results

PSD Slowing Confirmed: CONV group showed the classic slowing pattern: decreased beta power and increased low-frequency (theta/alpha) power compared to NOCONV.

Events Underlie Averaged Power: Both alpha and beta oscillations manifested as transient events in non-averaged data. Beta event features strongly correlated with averaged beta power.

Key Biomarker Finding: Only beta-event features distinguished CONV from NOCONV. CONV patients had:

  • Fewer beta events
  • Lower beta event power
  • Shorter beta event duration (especially in the Precuneus)

Predictive Power: Reduced beta event expression predicted higher odds of AD conversion within 2.5 years and was associated with poorer cognitive performance in the CONV group.

5. Discussion

Beta Events as a Novel Biomarker: The study establishes transient beta event features as sensitive biomarkers for early AD prediction, refining the traditional concept of oscillatory slowing.

Link to Inhibitory Control: The findings align with literature linking beta oscillations to inhibitory cognitive control. Reduced beta events in CONV may reflect a loss of inhibitory function, potentially leading to network hyperexcitability, a known mechanism in AD.

Why Alpha Events Didn't Differ: Despite group differences in averaged alpha power, alpha event features did not predict conversion. This may indicate that alpha slowing in later MCI stages involves more stationary signal changes not captured by the transient event analysis.

Mechanistic Hypothesis: The reduction in cortical beta events may stem from diminished thalamocortical drive, leading to reduced recruitment of cortical inhibition and consequent hyperexcitability in AD progression.

Future Directions: Highlights the potential for event-based metrics to bridge human biomarkers and cellular-level disease mechanisms, guiding therapeutic development.
